Nvidia (NASDAQ:NVDA) is getting little love from the market right now, even though investors could have hardly asked for a better performance from the chip giant in its FQ4 earnings report.It was casually excellent on all fronts once again and featured the kind of all-round beats investors have become accustomed to. The stock, however, subsequently dropped and has pulled back by 10% over the past two sessions. In fact, as Morgan Stanley analyst Joseph Moore points out, the shares have been unable to generate any momentum in recent months. “For the last two quarters NVIDIA has not moved while business has continued to strengthen – a function of concerns about the durability of current growth. Those concerns should turn to 2027 enthusiasm in the coming months,” Moore, who ranks among the top 2% of Street stock experts, explained.The bear case is that hyperscalers’ negative cash flow could limit future growth. That’s not entirely unfounded – some cloud infrastructure businesses do run at a loss – but strong balance sheets and the ability to raise cash help cushion the risk. Spending might slow a bit next year, yet cloud companies are likely to make more from these investments than most forecasts expect, especially with profitable cloud GPU businesses and growing supply-demand gaps. Meanwhile, frontier model developers want far more compute than the market can actually provide, thanks to semiconductor limits, while global supply chains show companies are paying upfront to secure growth, with no sign of a 2026 peak. “So we don’t buy into hundreds of gigawatts in 2029,” says Moore, “but we also don’t see a peak in 2026, and our growth expectations for CY27 grow seemingly by the week.”
		Moore points out that over the past three years, there has consistently been early-year skepticism about the year ahead. Yet each time, as clarity emerged and the underlying strength became apparent, NVDA stock experienced periods of strong outperformance. “Some year those concerns may be right, but our conviction is pretty high that this pattern will repeat,” the analyst added.Another concern revolves around eroding market share. The analyst does expect Nvidia’s peers to grow a bit faster in CY26, largely because it’s harder for Nvidia to sustain rapid growth when approaching $80 billion per quarter amid supply constraints. As a result, Nvidia’s market share could slip slightly, maybe by a point or two this year.It’s also true the moats have weakened somewhat as frontier model developers take an architecturally flexible approach, using Nvidia, AMD, TPUs, and internal ASICs. Still, Nvidia remains preferred in many cases, and Moore’s checks suggest the largest ASIC and AMD users are each likely to grow their Nvidia-based business by over 80% in CY26. “Market share concerns will be addressed at the coming GTC launch (March 16–19),” says the analyst, “demonstrating a leadership roadmap that again puts NVIDIA ahead of competition.”With Moore claiming the stock now offers a “surprisingly good entry point,” it is once again a “top pick” in semis (taking Micron’s place). To this end, Moore maintained an Overweight (i.e., Buy) rating on the shares backed by a $260 price target, a figure that points toward one-year gains of 47%.
