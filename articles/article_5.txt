OpenAI CEO Sam Altman admitted that the AI firm’s deal with the U.S. Defense Department looked “opportunistic and sloppy,” especially since it came shortly after the Pentagon clashed with rival Anthropic. Facing backlash over potential risks like domestic mass surveillance, OpenAI and the Pentagon are revising their contract. The department has also reaffirmed its commitment to civil liberties, pledging not to use the technology for spying on Americans.In a post on X, Altman announced that OpenAI is working with the department to strengthen their agreement. Key updates explicitly prohibit the use of AI for domestic surveillance of Americans, autonomous weapons, or services to intelligence agencies like the National Security Agency (NSA). Any future work with such agencies would require separate contract amendments.On Friday, February 27, OpenAI signed an agreement allowing its AI models to operate on the Pentagon’s classified networks. This positioned OpenAI as a key defense tech provider after Anthropic withdrew.
		The initial deal did not explicitly ban AI for domestic surveillance or require human oversight for lethal force, such as in autonomous weapons. It simply mandated compliance with existing U.S. laws, some of which have previously enabled broad domestic surveillance programs.While OpenAI and Anthropic are private companies, investors can choose to invest in one of several public AI players.
