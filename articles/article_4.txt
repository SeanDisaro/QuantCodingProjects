Catch up on the top artificial intelligence news and commentary by Wall Street analysts on publicly traded companies in the space with this daily recap compiled by The Fly.GTC KEYNOTE: Nvidia (NVDA) kicked off its GTC keynote with CEO Jensen Huang declaring that the “inference inflection” has arrived. The executive said the company now sees at least $1T in demand through 2027 for its data center AI chips, double what he projected a year ago. The keynote also delivered a sweeping slate of product launches, positioning Nvidia to power the next generation of AI factories and applications. Wall Street remained bullish on the stock following the keynote speech, with Cantor Fitzgerald saying Nvidia is still a Top Pick and BofA telling investors it sees continued Nvidia leadership in AI.Nvidia also unveiled DLSS 5, calling it “the company’s most significant breakthrough in computer graphics since the debut of real-time ray tracing in 2018.” It also said the Nvidia Vera Rubin platform is “opening the next frontier of agentic AI, with seven new chips now in full production to scale the world’s largest AI factories.” Among the other announcements were the launch of the Nvidia Vera CPU, space computing, the Nvidia NemoClaw stack for the OpenClaw agent platform, the Nvidia Nemotron Coalition, Nvidia Dynamo 1.0, and the Nvidia Physical AI Data Factory Blueprint.
		AWS DEAL: OpenAI on Friday signed a new contract with Amazon Web Services to sell its AI to U.S. government employees for both classified and unclassified work, according to two people with direct knowledge of the agreement, The Information’s Sri Muppidi and Aaron Holmes report. The contract will allow OpenAI to support the Pentagon through a deal it hastily won late last month after the agency ditched its current AI provider, Anthropic. AWS is already a large Cloud provider to numerous U.S. agencies and has agreed to sell OpenAI products to other U.S. government customers, these people said.‘NAIL’ CORE BUSINESS: OpenAI’s top executives are planning a major strategy shift to refocus the company around coding and business users, Berber Jin of The Wall Street Journal reports. In a recent all-hands meeting, Fidji Simo, OpenAI’s CEO of applications, previewed the changes to employees, telling them top leaders including CEO Sam Altman were looking at what areas could be deprioritized. “We cannot miss this moment because we are distracted by side quests,” Simo told staff last week, according to the Journal. “We really have to nail productivity in general and particularly productivity on the business front.”AREAS TO DE-PRIORITIZE: Fidji Simo, OpenAI’s CEO of applications, has previewed company changes to employees in an all-hands meeting, indicating that the company’s top management – including CEO Sam Altman and chief research officer Mark Chen – are actively looking to deprioritize some areas of the business, The Wall Street Journal’s Berber Jin reports. The top executives are said to believe that a “do everything all at once” strategy has put them on the defensive, and the company should refocus on coding and business users.PERSONAL INTELLIGENCE: Google (GOOGL) said in a blog post that Personal Intelligence is expanding in the U.S. across AI Mode in Search, the Gemini app, and Gemini in Chrome. “Personal Intelligence allows you to securely connect the dots across your Google apps – like Gmail, Google Photos and more – to provide responses that are uniquely relevant to you,” the company said. “Whether you’re looking for a specific brand of sneakers you previously purchased, or planning a family getaway based on your hotel confirmations and past travel memories, Personal Intelligence helps you find exactly what you need without having to give all the context.” Personal Intelligence is available Tuesday in the U.S. for AI Mode in Search and it’s starting to roll out in the Gemini app and Gemini in Chrome for free-tier users.
