Nvidia (NASDAQ:NVDA) stock might be showing little momentum right now but if history is anything to go by, that might all change soon.The chip giant’s upcoming GTC 2026 event is scheduled for March 16‑19. Historically, the stock has tended to perform strongly after GTC, with Nvidia typically outperforming the SOX index by around 30% over the three months following the event, ranging from roughly 12% to 45% over the past few years.Hence, says Wells Fargo analyst Aaron Rakers: “We’re NVDA buyers ahead of the event.”
		Among the items Rakers will be looking out for are updates regarding the pipeline. With commitments already exceeding $500 billion and potentially stretching into calendar 2027, Rakers estimates Nvidia has recognized roughly $240 billion to $250 billion of that opportunity since Blackwell began shipping in late 2024. An update to $600 billion+ would be a positive.Additionally, Rakers, who ranks among the top 1% of analysts on Wall Street, will be watching for updates on Nvidia’s move to the Kyber architecture – its next-generation, high-density rack design – and how lessons from the early GB200 NVL72 rollout might shape that transition. He’ll also be looking to see whether Feynman, Nvidia’s planned post-Rubin GPU architecture, represents a completely new approach or just an evolution of existing designs. Rakers will also be keeping an eye on Rubin CPX, Nvidia’s GPU built for long‑context AI inference and expected to launch in late 2026, as well as how the company positions its Groq LPU for AI workloads. He’s looking for more detail on Rubin CPX’s architecture and how Nvidia plans to integrate Groq’s technology, particularly its large on-chip SRAM and streaming design that helps keep inference fast and low-latency.There could be some “wildcards” thrown in the mix too. The 5-star analyst points out that AI infrastructure growth reflects Jevons Paradox – as compute gets more powerful, usage expands, driving more token generation. Within this context, Groq integration vs. new inference-focused silicon could be in focus. There has been speculation about whether Nvidia’s Groq IP agreement will lead to standalone chips or if the technology will be incorporated into the company’s future GPU roadmap.Another wildcard focuses on the prospect of customization. AMD + Meta’s recent chiplet-based partnership highlights the potential for custom AI hardware for hyperscalers. Nvidia’s Blackwell and Rubin GPUs already use a chiplet/dielet architecture, with Vera-Rubin Ultra expanding this further. The Wall Street Journal has reported that Nvidia plans a dedicated inference chip for OpenAI, leveraging its Groq IP license to optimize AI inference. According to the report, OpenAI has committed to significant purchases, making customized AI hardware a potential wildcard for Nvidia’s competitive positioning.
		All told, Rakers maintained an Overweight (i.e., Buy) rating on the shares, backed by a $265 price target. The implication for investors? Upside of 47% from current levels.
