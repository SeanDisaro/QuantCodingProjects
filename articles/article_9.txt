ChatGPT-maker OpenAI listed its reliance on Microsoft (MSFT) as a top risk in an investor document ahead of a potential IPO (initial public offering), per CNBC. The company relies on Microsoft for “a substantial portion of our financing and compute.” Any change or termination of the partnership could harm OpenAI’s “business, prospects, operating results, and financial condition.”This prospectus-like document circulated during OpenAI’s latest funding round. In February, it raised $110 billion from strategic partners including Amazon (AMZN), Nvidia (NVDA), and SoftBank (SFTBY). The company is now reportedly partnering with banks to add another $10 billion in commitments from a wider group of investors, expected to close by the end of March.Microsoft has been one of the earliest backers of OpenAI, having invested up to $13 billion since 2019. During OpenAI’s restructuring in October, Microsoft disclosed that its 27% diluted stake in the for-profit entity was valued at $135 billion.
		OpenAI noted that its success depends on building ties beyond Microsoft. If Microsoft ends the partnership or OpenAI cannot diversify partners, its business, outlook, results, and finances could suffer. A spokesperson called this standard disclosure, stressing Microsoft remains a vital partner.Yet, over the years, the two have turned into AI rivals as they compete for the same enterprise customers. Microsoft also lists OpenAI as a competitor, and OpenAI has turned to other cloud providers like CoreWeave (CRWV), Google (GOOGL), and Oracle (ORCL) to diversify its risks.OpenAI flags geopolitical threats, including chip shortages that could cripple its compute-heavy models. Tensions over Taiwan might disrupt Taiwan Semiconductor (TSM) supplies. The company plans massive spending on compute, data centers, and infrastructure with partners like Microsoft, Nvidia, AMD (AMD), and Broadcom (AVGO). By December, it had $665 billion in compute commitments through 2030, and needs could grow much more.It also warns of lawsuits over copyrights, patents, IP, jobs, contracts, privacy, and more. OpenAI faces three lawsuits from co-founder Elon Musk (or his xAI), who left in 2018 after a failed Tesla (TSLA) merger push and has sued since 2024. Plus, 14 California suits from ChatGPT users’ families allege mental illness, suicide, death, or injury.Notably, OpenAI omits CEO Sam Altman from key personnel risks, despite his central role and 2023 ouster/reinstatement amid backlash.
