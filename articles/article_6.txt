Nvidia (NVDA) shares edged lower on Tuesday after U.S. lawmakers moved to block the company’s AI chip exports to China and parts of Southeast Asia. The move follows a recent case in which Nvidia chips were allegedly routed to China through third countries.The push comes after the indictment of Super Micro Computer (SMCI) co-founder Wally Liaw, who is accused of bypassing U.S. export controls.Lawmakers pointed to the Super Micro case as evidence that current monitoring systems may not be strong enough. In a letter to the Commerce Department, U.S. senators urged officials to pause or suspend export licenses for advanced Nvidia chips, citing the risk that the technology could still reach China through intermediaries.
		They also raised questions about earlier statements from Nvidia’s CEO Jensen Huang, who had assured officials that customers “monitor themselves very carefully” to prevent illegal diversion. Lawmakers said the scale of the issue suggests gaps in monitoring and compliance.In response, Nvidia said following export rules remains a top priority and that it continues to work with customers and the government. The company added that it does not provide support for systems that are diverted illegally.This escalation comes just as the Trump administration had begun issuing licenses for Nvidia’s H200 chips to China. At the same time, the House Foreign Affairs Committee is set to vote on the Chip Security Act this week, which could tighten controls further.For investors, the key question is whether export rules will become stricter. Any change could affect Nvidia’s access to key markets and its growth outlook in the near term.Nvidia’s stock has a consensus Strong Buy rating among 42 Wall Street analysts. That rating is based on 41 Buy and one Hold recommendations issued in the past three months. The average NVDA price target of $273.34 implies 56% upside from current levels.
