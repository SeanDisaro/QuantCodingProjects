Lawyers representing authors & publishers in a $1.5 billion Anthropic copyright settlement have been forced to slash their legal fee after a U.S. district court judge criticized their original $300 million request as “oversized.” Following challenges to their proposed fee-sharing arrangement, the legal team, led by Susman Godfrey and Lieff Cabraser, filed a revised bid on March 19, reducing their request to $187.5 million.The fee dispute followed objections from Anthropic and two federal judges overseeing the copyright lawsuit. Before retiring in late 2025, Judge William Alsup, who initially presided over the lawsuit, issued a Dec. 23 memorandum challenging efforts to allocate fees to three outside firms, Cowan DeBaets Abrahams & Sheppard, Edelson, and Oppenheim + Zebrak, that had not been appointed as class counsel.The case was reassigned in early 2026 to Judge Araceli Martínez-Olguín, who upheld that position, stating during a Jan. 22 hearing that any attorneys outside the two lead firms were “working for free.” Separately, Anthropic argued there was “scant justification or substantiation” for the fees sought by the non-class counsel firms. Facing continued scrutiny from both the judges and Anthropic, the lead lawyers revised their request, cutting the total fee and dropping a previously proposed $75 million allocation for the excluded firms.
		The settlement resolves claims that Anthropic used copyrighted books to train its Claude AI chatbot without permission, leading to a class action filed as Bartz v. Anthropic PBC. The lawsuit, initiated in 2024 by authors Andrea Bartz, Charles Graeber, and Kirk Wallace Johnson, alleged that Anthropic downloaded nearly 500,000 books from pirate databases, including Library Genesis and Pirate Library Mirror, to train its AI.  Facing potential statutory damages in the tens of billions at trial, Anthropic agreed in Aug. 2025 to a $1.5 billion settlement, providing roughly $3,000 per copyrighted work. The agreement also requires the company to destroy all pirated copies in its possession, with each named plaintiff to receive $50,000, and $18 million allocated to the settlement administrator. Anthropic’s defense includes Cooley LLP, Arnold & Porter, Latham & Watkins, Lex Lumina LLP, and Morrison & Foerster. A final approval hearing for the settlement and the revised fee request is scheduled for April 23, 2026, before Martínez-Olguín in San Francisco. No, Anthropic is a private company. While an initial public offering (IPO) is anticipated in 2026, its shares are not publicly available yet.
