The “SaaSpocalypse” has sent Salesforce (CRM) shares into a tailspin, as investors weigh the existential threat posed by AI agents to traditional seat-based software. While fears of a structural shift in enterprise licensing are valid, I firmly believe the current market panic overlooks the company’s massive distribution advantage and the fact that it is trading at its most attractive valuation in years. With top-line growth re-accelerating and a record buyback program in place, the downside appears aggressively priced in.Consequently, I maintain a firm bullish outlook on CRM.To understand the bearish thesis, you have to look at the “death of the seat” narrative. For 20 years, Salesforce grew by selling licenses. That’s one seat for one human. However, we are entering the era of the “agentic enterprise,” in which a single AI agent can theoretically do the work of 10 junior account executives. If a company can automate its entire sales funnel using a lean, AI-native startup or a custom-built tool from Anthropic or OpenAI, why keep paying thousands per month for a bloated CRM suite?
		The bears are right to point out a genuine structural shift. We’ve seen the “SaaSpocalypse” play out in real time as players like Anthropic release plugins that handle legal compliance and document review. These are tasks that used to require specialized, high-margin software. The fear is that Salesforce is a “system of record” in a world that now demands “systems of action.” If someone can replicate Salesforce’s data model with less complexity and pair it with a smoother AI-driven experience, the company’s long-standing advantage starts to feel a lot less secure. Investors are valuing CRM as though its core business is gradually losing momentum, and in the near term, that skepticism is not entirely misplaced.In my view, the whole idea that AI will simply take over software leaves out an important piece of the puzzle. It ignores distribution and the pull of data. Some people talk as if you can aim an AI at a company and tell it to sell more, and that it will just happen. It does not work that way. You need years of historical data, real customer relationships, and tightly connected workflows, all of which Salesforce has been building for decades.This is exactly what Nvidia (NVDA) CEO Jensen Huang was getting at recently when he pushed back on the software doom-and-gloom. Huang argued that “the markets got it wrong,” explaining that AI agents are “tool users,” not tool destroyers. An AI agent still needs a platform to work within, and Salesforce is arguably the most robust platform ever built for the front office. Instead of being disrupted, Salesforce is becoming the orchestrator.The recent rollout of Agentforce, which saw ARR hit $800 million in Q4 Fiscal 2026, signals a pivot from selling human seats to selling “digital labor.” Leveraging its vast distribution network, they are implementing AI at a scale that startups simply cannot match. When Salesforce CEO Marc Benioff says that the “SaaSpocalypse” is actually a marketing opportunity, he’s pointing to the fact that his customers are looking to make their existing data smart. Performance obligations hitting new records reflect this.
		Now, the stock has been absolutely mauled lately, yet the underlying growth is actually picking up speed. In the fourth quarter of Fiscal 2026 (ending January 2026), Salesforce posted revenue growth of 12%, a significant milestone because it marks the first real re-acceleration we’ve seen since the post-pandemic hangover. It’s actually the best quarter in terms of growth since Q4 Fiscal 2023 (ending January 2023).For Fiscal 2027, Salesforce is expected to post adjusted EPS of $13.21. At current price levels, that implies a forward P/E ratio of just 14.7x. To put that in perspective, this is one of the cheapest valuations in the company’s entire history as a public entity. This reminds me of “Big Tobacco” or “Legacy-Utility” valuation days for a company that is still the undisputed leader in enterprise cloud.Management clearly sees the disconnect, too, since they authorized a $50 billion share buyback. It seems like they know the market is mispricing the durability of their cash flow. Today, that $50 billion buyback is nearly 28% of Salesforce’s market cap, which could turn the tables in the bears-versus-bulls arena. Also, if and when the narrative eventually shifts from “AI is killing Salesforce” to “AI is making Salesforce more profitable,” it’s safe to say the re-rating will be violent and rewarding.On Wall Street, Salesforce stock carries a Moderate Buy consensus rating based on 29 Buy, 10 Hold, and only one Sell rating. In addition, CRM’s average stock price target of $275.32 implies about 41% upside potential over the next 12 months.It’s still only early March, yet the “SaaSpocalypse” is already the ultimate red herring this year. While I do agree that the transition from human seats to AI agents could be messy, there is no doubt that Salesforce has the data and the scale to emerge as the definitive winner. In the meantime, the stock’s valuation appears to form a wide margin of safety following its prolonged sell-off. If you believe in the power of incumbency and the reality of the numbers, the current price could be a gift for patient investors.
