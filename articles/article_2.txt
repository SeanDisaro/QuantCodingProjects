U.S. stocks closed lower for the week as war risk in the Gulf and a weak jobs report shook the market. The S&P 500 (SPX) fell 1.98%, while the Nasdaq 100 (NDX) slipped 1.24%. The Dow Jones (DJIA) lost 2.95%. The 10-year Treasury yield rose to 4.14%. At the same time, gold (CM:XAUUSD) climbed to $5,173, oil (CM:CL) surged to $91.27, and Bitcoin (BTC-USD) fell near $68,000.Even so, beneath the index level, the action was sharp. Energy stocks rose with oil, while travel and cyclicals lagged as fuel costs jumped and jobs data cooled hopes for fast growth.First, the main story came from the Gulf. War risk near the Strait of Hormuz hit ship flows and shook the oil trade. Tanker traffic fell near 90% as ship firms halted or rerouted cargo. Oil then logged a record weekly surge of more than 35% and moved near $90 per barrel.
		As a result, energy shares gained. Exxon Mobil Corporation (XOM) and Chevron Corporation (CVX) both rose as traders priced in a tighter oil supply.At the same time, gas markets also felt the shock. Qatar halted some LNG exports after drone strikes, which pushed gas prices in Europe up as much as 50%. U.S. LNG firm Cheniere Energy (LNG) rose as traders looked for new demand from Europe and Asia.Yet the oil surge also raised concern about costs and growth. Airlines fell as fuel prices jumped. United Airlines Holdings (UAL) and Delta Air Lines (DAL) both slid late in the week.Moreover, the U.S. jobs report added to the caution. The economy lost 92,000 jobs in February, far worse than the 55,000 gain economists had expected. The jobless rate rose to 4.4%, while job gains for the prior two months were cut by 69,000.Omair Sharif of Inflation Insights said the data shows a weak labor trend. “This is about a labor market that is so soft that it cannot withstand a strike of 31,000 physicians in health care, because no one else is hiring.”
		Soon after the report, traders raised the odds of at least one Fed rate cut in June to about 67%, up from less than 40% before the data.Meanwhile, the White House also named Kevin Warsh as its pick for the next Fed chair. The move may shape policy views as the Fed weighs weak jobs data against new price risk from oil.Even so, tech and AI news still drove key moves in the market. First, Nvidia Corporation (NVDA) posted a record quarter and announced plans to secure supply for new AI data centers. The firm will invest $2 billion each in Coherent Corp. (COHR) and Lumentum Holdings Inc. (LITE) to boost optical network gear used in high-speed AI systems.Next, Broadcom Inc. (AVGO) also beat views for the quarter. The chip firm said AI revenue rose to $8.4 billion, more than double last year’s level. Broadcom also set a $10 billion buyback plan and guided the next quarter to about $22 billion in revenue.At the same time, major tech firms moved to deal with a new limit in the AI boom. Microsoft Corporation (MSFT), Alphabet Inc. (GOOGL), Amazon (AMZN), Meta Platforms (META), and Oracle Corporation (ORCL) signed a White House pledge to fund new power supply and grid upgrades tied to AI data centers. The aim is to ensure that local power rates do not rise as AI demand grows.
		Meanwhile, policy risk also stayed in view. U.S. officials are weighing new export terms that could link AI chip sales by Nvidia and Advanced Micro Devices (AMD) to foreign investment pledges.As the new week begins, traders will watch both the war in the Gulf and the path of oil prices. First, markets will track tanker traffic through the Strait of Hormuz. Any sign that ship flows are resuming could ease pressure on oil prices and calm energy markets.Next, investors will watch oil itself. If crude stays near $90 or moves higher, inflation fears may return, affecting rate views.In addition, traders will study the next wave of economic data for signs that the labor market is cooling further after the weak February report.Finally, AI spending will remain a key theme for tech stocks. Updates from chip firms and cloud groups may show how long strong AI demand can support the sector even as the macro-outlook shifts.
